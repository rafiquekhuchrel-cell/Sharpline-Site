A new client doesn't judge you on your best work in month three. They judge you in the first two weeks, before you've had a chance to prove anything — based entirely on how organized the process feels. Get that part wrong, and even great work later doesn't fully undo the first impression.

Here's where it actually goes wrong, and what fixes it.

## Mistake 1: no defined moment between "signed" and "started"

The contract is signed. Then... what? If the honest answer is "I start whenever I get around to it," the client is left wondering if they made a mistake. The fix is a defined Day 0 sequence: send the signed copy back for their records, send the deposit invoice, get them into whatever system you track projects in. None of this is glamorous, but it's the difference between a client who feels handled and one who's quietly worried.

## Mistake 2: the kickoff call has no agenda

"Let's hop on a call to kick things off" without a structure wastes the single best trust-building opportunity you have. A real kickoff call covers four things every time: confirm the goals in the client's own words, walk through what's in scope and — just as important — what's explicitly not in scope, confirm how you'll communicate and how fast they can expect replies, and set the check-in cadence so there's never a moment where they're wondering if you disappeared.

## Mistake 3: asking for access piecemeal

Nothing signals disorganization like a new request for login access every few days for two weeks. Send one onboarding questionnaire, request everything you'll need at once — website, analytics, brand assets, whatever the project requires — and confirm it all works before you start, not mid-project when you discover something's missing.

## Mistake 4: check-ins with no structure

"Just checking in!" with no actual content is worse than no update at all — it signals there's nothing to report. A real status update covers four things, every time, in the same order: what's done, what's in progress, what's next, and anything blocking progress. Clients don't need more communication. They need communication they can predict the shape of.

## Mistake 5: no real offboarding

The project ends, files get sent, and that's it. This is the most commonly skipped stage, and it's the one most likely to generate a referral or a review if you do it well. A real close includes walking the client through anything they now own or need to manage themselves, a final invoice, and — while the work is freshest in their mind and they're feeling good about it — asking directly for a testimonial or review.

## Why this is a system problem, not a memory problem

None of these mistakes happen because someone doesn't care. They happen because there's no written process, so every client gets a slightly different, slightly improvised version — and improvisation is exactly what shows under pressure, on your busiest week, with your most important client.
