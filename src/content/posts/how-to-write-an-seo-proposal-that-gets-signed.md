Most SEO proposals fail for a reason that has nothing to do with the SEO work itself: they open with a pitch instead of a diagnosis. A prospect who hasn't been convinced you understand their problem isn't going to trust your solution — no matter how good your pricing tiers look.

Here's the structure that actually closes, and why each part exists.

## Start with findings, not features

Before you write a single word about deliverables, show the prospect what you found. Not generic SEO talking points — their actual site, their actual issues.

A findings section should cover four areas:

- **Technical** — Core Web Vitals, crawl errors, indexation issues
- **Content** — keyword gaps, thin or missing pages for high-intent terms
- **Schema** — missing structured data that's costing them rich results
- **Authority** — backlink profile compared to their real competitors

This does two things at once. It proves you actually looked at their site instead of sending a templated pitch, and it reframes the whole proposal — you're not selling a service, you're presenting a diagnosis they now want fixed.

## Scope it by month, not by vague promise

"Ongoing SEO services" is the single weakest phrase you can put in a proposal. It tells the client nothing about what happens after they pay. Replace it with an actual month-by-month breakdown:

- **Month 1** — technical foundation: fix the Core Web Vitals and indexation issues you found
- **Month 2** — content and on-page: close the keyword gaps with real pages
- **Month 3** — authority and reporting: outreach campaign, first full performance report

Specificity here isn't just clearer — it's a trust signal. Clients who've been burned by vague retainers before will notice the difference immediately.

## Price in tiers, and guide them to the middle one

Three tiers, not one flat price. A single price forces a yes/no decision; three tiers turn it into a "which one" decision, which converts better in almost every service category. Structure it as Basic / Standard / Full-Service, and visually flag the middle tier — that's usually where your margin is best, and framing effects are well-documented enough that most buyers gravitate there anyway when it's marked as the recommended option.

## Set expectations before they ask

The fastest way to lose a client three weeks in is a stated or implied promise that rankings move in week one. They don't. Say so directly:

- **Weeks 1–4** — foundation work, rankings typically flat
- **Months 2–3** — early movement on lower-competition terms
- **Months 4–6** — where compounding growth actually becomes visible

This isn't just honesty for its own sake — a client who knows month one is "flat, that's expected" doesn't panic and churn in month one. You've pre-answered the question before it becomes a complaint.

## Close with a decision, not a maybe

End with a clear next step and a real signature block — not "let me know if you have questions." A proposal that ends in ambiguity gets replied to eventually, or never. A proposal that ends in "sign here or reply to confirm" gets a decision.
