Ask ten founders if they have a financial forecast, and most of them will show you a spreadsheet that's actually a budget. The two get used interchangeably, but they answer completely different questions — and only one of them can tell you whether your business actually works.

## What a budget actually does

A budget is a plan for spending. It says: this month, rent is $500, software is $200, marketing is $800. It's useful, and every business needs one — but a budget has no opinion about revenue. It doesn't know if you'll make $5,000 or $50,000 this month. It just tracks what goes out.

## What a forecast actually does

A forecast is a model of your whole business over time. Revenue assumptions feed into a profit and loss statement. The P&L feeds into cash flow — because profit on paper and cash in the bank are not the same thing, and the gap between them is where most small businesses actually run into trouble. Cash flow feeds into a balance sheet, which should balance to zero if the model is built correctly.

Change one input — your pricing, your growth rate, your churn — and every number downstream should move with it. That's the actual test of whether you're looking at a forecast or a very well-formatted guess: does changing one assumption ripple through the whole model, or does everything just sit there?

## Why this distinction matters more than it sounds like it should

Three concrete situations where the difference is the whole ballgame:

**Applying for a loan or pitching an investor.** Lenders and investors aren't reading your budget. They're asking whether the business, modeled honestly, can service debt or return capital. A budget can't answer that question. Only a forecast with linked cash flow can.

**Knowing when you'll be tight on cash.** Profitable companies run out of cash constantly — it's one of the most common ways healthy-looking businesses actually fail. A budget won't warn you. A cash flow forecast, built off realistic collection timing (not "invoice sent = money in the bank the same day"), will show you the exact month it gets tight, while you still have time to do something about it.

**Knowing your real break-even point.** Not "what's my monthly revenue target" in the abstract, but the actual number where your contribution margin covers your fixed costs — and the actual month you'll hit it, given your real growth rate, not a hoped-for one.

## The trap: a "forecast" that's just a formatted budget

The most common failure mode isn't having no financial model — it's having one that looks like a forecast but behaves like a budget. Static numbers typed into cells that look like a 3-year projection, but change nothing when you change an assumption. It creates false confidence, which is arguably worse than having no model at all, because you think you've done the work.

The test is simple: pick one assumption — your growth rate, your pricing, your starting cash — and change it. If your Year 3 revenue, your break-even month, and your ending cash balance don't all move in response, you don't have a forecast. You have a nicely formatted guess.
